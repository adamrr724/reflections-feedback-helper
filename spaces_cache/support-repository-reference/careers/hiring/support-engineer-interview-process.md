## Cover letter and resume review

Ideally, candidates should supply a cover letter in addition to a resume. A cover letter can illustrate the candidate's ability to write clearly.  Be aware that resumes can introduce bias in some areas. For instance, where the candidate went to school and where they've worked may introduce bias.  However, work history and project experience on the resume can provide an overview of the candidate's history and skills.  Review both items if provided, and try to be aware of and combat unconscious bias here and in every step of the interview process.

### Reviewing a cover letter

When reading a cover letter, these are the primary things to look for:
- Clarity in written communication
- Focus on customer service
- Systems, development, virtualization, etc.. experience
- Entry level experience handling technical support issues
- Ability to work cross functionally with various teams

If the candidate seems simply excited about working at GitHub, and does not specifically talk about support, customer service, etc.. they should be rejected.

## Hiring Manager Interview (Support Engineer)

This is the primary interview between the hiring manager and the candidate. It is the only opportunity the manager will get to speak with the candidate directly and should cover some basic areas regarding customer service, technical experience and support experience.

### Time

60 minutes

### Prep

The interviewer should prepare by:
- Reviewing the candidate's cover letter and resume
- Reviewing the interview questions

### Interview introduction

Begin the interview by introducing yourself and your role at the company. Talk briefly about the purpose of the GitHub Support team and the Support Engineer role. Points to highlight:

- We are one part of the larger Support organization, focused on GitHub Enterprise customers.
- The team's purpose is to provide standard support (ticket/portal) and premium support (phone support/escalation management).
- The primary responsibilities of the Support Engineer role is to answer customer technical support requests. 
- The Support Engineer will work with other parts of the support team, sales, and engineering.
- This position does require on-call rotation work, phone coverage and weekend work. Confirm that the candidate is willing to participate in these requirements. 

This should take no more than three minutes or so, but it helps give some context for the candidate.

### Interview questions and expected answers

- Have the candidate talk through their technical background and/or have them describe their current job responsibilities. Ask follow up questions geared toward level 2-3 support here at GitHub. The idea is to get a better understanding if the candidate has the ability to troubleshoot and resolve non-entry level problems.

- Why do you want to work at GitHub?

  - __Good__
    - Freedom to work on a product you love and care about.
    - Enjoy the problems that we’re working on.
    - Likes the company values and culture.
    - Feels GitHub is a good fit for their background and can articulate why.
  - __Bad__
    - Just looking for a job and does not know much about the company or hasn't even bothered to do the research.
    - Even if they do not know about much about the product, should still have strong reasons to want to work at GitHub.

- What does your ideal career path look like?

  - __Good__ 
    - Recognizes how a background in Support can enrich work in other areas.
    - Likes being in Support and may want to try different roles within Support.
  - __Bad__
    - Sees it as entry level, wants to get foot in the door.

- Why do you want to work in customer-facing Support?

  - __Good__
    - Love working with people.
    - Enjoy working with customers and feels customer satisfaction is important.
  - __Bad__
    - More interested in development.
    - Does not want to be tied to a phone.
    - Prefers to work alone or interact with few people.

- What are the most important elements of a positive support interaction? 

  - __Good__
    - Quick and consistent communication with the customer.
    - Empathy and friendliness.
    - Timely resolution to question or issue.
    - Identifying and understanding the root of the issue.
  - __Bad__
    - Unable to articulate what good support is.
    - Doesn't emphasize communication and empathy.

- What would you consider your technical strength?

  - __Good__
    - Gives specific example and can articulate the strength. 
    - Picks something related to the role and would benefit the team (e.g. troubleshooting, quick learner, passionate). 
  - __Bad__
    - Can't think of a strength. 

- What experience have you had administering or troubleshooting Linux or Unix environments or issues?

  - __Good__
    - Has used Linux in previous roles.
    - Some experience and feels comfortable navigating around.
    - Knows what Linux is and has experience working in Linux.
    - Lists their favorite flavors of Linux.
  - __Bad__
    - Not familiar with Linux or never used it.
    - Can not name flavors of Linux.

- Describe your overall approach to troubleshooting a difficult technical problem.

  - __Good__
    - Does some independent research.
    - Leans on team for help when needed.
    - Treats problem, not just the symptoms.
  - __Bad__
    - Just Googles for answer.
    - Isn't able to articulate an answer.

- Have you ever encountered an issue maintaining relationships or communicating with co-workers in a remote environment? If so, how did you handle it?

  - __Good__
    - Communication tools/asynchronous communication are vital to the success of remote teams.
    - Talking to people in person once in a while and via Zoom, Skype, etc. is a good way to get a feel for how your coworkers are operating.
  - __Bad__
    - Doesn't believe remote teams can be effective.
    - Remote people need more baby-sitting.
    - Can only communicate via phone or email.
    - Is not motivated to collaborate and reach out to others.

- How do or would you manage distractions and stay focused throughout the day?

  - __Good__
    - Having a dedicated workspace.
    - Taking breaks as needed.
    - Using productivity tools - timers, focus tools, etc.
  - __Bad__
    - Hasn't given it any thought.

- Why is hiring a diverse workforce important in your view?

  - __Awesome__        
    - Bring in a personal example (work, class, volunteer, community)
    - Clear example, noting comfort describing the team's demographics. Clear articulation about the challenges and benefits of this team.
  - __Great__
    - Talking on a theoretical level (read a book, movie, class, etc.)
    - Passionate, genuine, curious.
  - __OK__
    - Come across as less genuine or enthusiastic about the topic.
    - Demonstrated desire to be part of a diverse team, regardless of past experience.
  - __Not So Great__
    - “It isn’t a concern of mine.”
    - Lack of interest/dismissive about the topic.
    - No interest, resistance to the question, negative expression about how it would be to work on a diverse team.

  - __Behaviours to look for__

    - different _style_ depending on content and recipient
  - __Good__
    - _Planned_ delivery carefully

    - _Rehearsed_ based on planning, message, and audience
    - Planning led to excellent _outcome_
    - Answers to questions _tailored_ to audience member asking
  - __Bad__
    - _Limited_ ways of communicating
    - _No tailoring_ for audience
    - Lack of message _planning_
    - Singular delivery not flexible

- Regularly we have to send written information to persuade customers or brief colleagues internally. Describe a situation when you had to write something to persuade others.
  - _An example might be, persuading a customer about steps they should undertake to prepare for a successful upgrade to prevent pitfalls leading to an outage._

   - __Behaviours to look for__
     - _structure_ their message based on situation?
     - apply a standard _template_?
     - How did they _review and edit_ their work?
     - Did they _plan for time_ to review, or delay until time became a factor?
   - __Good__
     - Clear _structure_ followed tailored for the message and objective
     - _Edited_ carefully and repeatedly
     - Encouraged _multiple inputs_ to help refine message
     - Considered audience and time in creation
     - Achieved objective
   - __Bad__
     - Limited plan or structure to writing
     - Numerous errors in tone, spelling, grammar
     - Poor planning led to excess length
     - No clarity of goal
     - Excessive use of non-standard vocabulary

- Sometimes decisions don’t go our way. Tell me about a time when you had to champion a decision which was not your idea. What did you feel and what did you do?

  - __Behaviours to look for__
    - openly acknowledge that their idea wasn’t going to be used, and that was acceptable?
    - actively support the idea, or think that simply not working against it was sufficient? 
    - Did they put-to-bed their alternative? Did they stop working on developing their own "parallel track" idea?
  - __Good__
    - Describes benefits of the idea chosen
    - Openly supports idea
    - Demonstrates action taken to support idea & ensure success
  - __Bad__
    - Supported idea grudgingly
    - Developed other ideas on the side
    - Expresses frustration/distress at idea not being used

- Frequently you’ll be able to choose the way you accomplish tasks. Other times, there may be precise guidance. Tell me about a time when you needed to follow instructions accurately. How did you ensure that your work was correct?
  - _For instance, migrating to new hardware or restoring from backup may require, attention to detail in following something step-by-step. If they're responsible, do they do a dry run, testing first and measuring the outcome against what's expected?_

  - __Behaviours to look for__
    - What did the candidate do to ensure they understood the instructions?
    - Did they write them down, or ask questions?
    - What steps did they take to ensure that the work didn’t get off track?
    - Did they do anything to make sure the final product was what was expected?
  - __Good__
    - Took notes
    - Asked for clarification
    - Open with communications about questions and issues
    - Validated assumptions
    - Planned quality into the work
    - Checked work for accuracy
  - __Bad__
    - Did not remember instructions
    - Did not ask questions or clarify
    - Made errors left uncorrected
    - Had to be prompted with repeated guidance
    - Hid errors
   
- Can you describe a time when you managed ongoing relationships with multiple customers simultaneously? How did you ensure that each customer felt valued and received timely support, and what strategies did you use to balance competing priorities and maintain effective communication?
  - __Good__
    - Evidence of organized processes, tracking tools, or specific methodologies for managing customer interactions.
    - Examples of regular updates, scheduled check-ins, and proactive outreach.
    - Clear strategies for prioritizing tasks and managing competing demands, such as categorizing issues based on urgency and impact.
    - Instances where the candidate has sought and acted on customer feedback or used it to improve service.
    - Specific examples of successful relationship management, including positive outcomes and feedback from customers.
  - __Bad__
    - Responses that indicate a lack of system or structure for managing multiple customers, such as relying solely on memory or ad-hoc responses.
    - Answers that suggest the candidate only addresses issues as they arise without proactive planning or regular communication.
    - Statements that show difficulty in balancing competing demands or lack of clear strategies for handling urgent versus non-urgent issues.
    - A lack of emphasis on regular updates, follow-ups, or systematic communication.
    - Answers that are overly general or lack specific examples or strategies.
- Can you give an example of how you built a strong relationship with a challenging customer? What strategies did you use to understand their needs and address their concerns?  
  - __Good__
    - The candidate describes how they actively listened to the customer's concerns and demonstrated empathy.
    - Specific strategies tailored to the customer’s unique situation, such as customizing solutions or adapting communication styles.
    - Details on how the candidate identified the root of the customer's challenges and developed effective solutions.
    - Evidence of follow-up actions taken to ensure the customer’s ongoing satisfaction and to maintain the relationship.
    - Results or feedback that indicate the relationship improved over time and the customer became more satisfied or loyal.
  - __Bad__
    - Vague or general answers without concrete examples or details about how the relationship was built.
    - An emphasis on dealing with issues only as they arose, without proactive measures to understand or address the customer’s needs.
    - Responses that place blame on the customer for being challenging or difficult, rather than focusing on how they adapted to the customer’s needs.
    - Absence of mention of follow-up actions or steps taken to ensure ongoing customer satisfaction.
    - Responses that place blame on the customer for being challenging or difficult, rather than focusing on how they adapted to the customer’s needs.

- We are Premium Plus, which is part of the larger Support organization, focused on GitHub Premium customers.
- The Customer Reliability Engineer's purpose 
  - Customer Relationship Management: Align with assigned accounts while building and maintaining collaborative/strategic relationships.

## Codility Technical Assessment

**Format:** Codility (asynchronous)  
**Duration:** 60 minutes  
**Benchmark Score:** 60%

Candidates complete a 10-question multiple choice assessment covering CI/CD troubleshooting, code security, GitHub Actions, Copilot usage and privacy, and GHES diagnostics.

### Scoring

- **Multiple-response questions** — Strict matching: candidate must select exactly the correct options to get points
- **Automatically scored by Codility**
- **Benchmark:** 60% to pass

For full question details and correct answers, see the [Codility Assessment Guide](technical-assessment/codility-assessment.md).

## Technical Debrief

**GitHub Values Assessed:** Customer-Obsessed, Ship to Learn, Growth Mindset, Own the Outcome, Better Together

A 45-minute live interview where candidates discuss their reasoning behind their answers to the multiple choice technical assessment they completed asynchronously. The interviewer probes the candidate's understanding, diagnostic methodology, safety awareness, and communication skills.

### Debrief Goals (What We Validate)

- **Authenticity:** Does the candidate truly understand their answers and can explain them in their own words and outline next steps?
- **Technical Safety:** Does the candidate avoid risky or destructive actions and show awareness of safe troubleshooting?
- **Practicality:** Are the candidate's steps logical, efficient, and realistic for solving the problem in a real-world setting?

### Time

45 minutes with candidate + ~15 minutes after interview to enter feedback into iCIMS

### Prep

The interviewer should prepare by:
- Reviewing the candidate's multiple choice assessment responses
- Reviewing the [Technical Debrief Run Sheet](technical-debrief/runsheet.md)

### Interview Flow

| Time | Section |
|------|---------|
| 0–5 min | Set context — "We'll walk through some of the questions in the Technical Assessment done earlier, validate reasoning, and discuss how you arrived at the answer." |
| 5–10 min | Candidate summary — "In 2–3 minutes, summarize your approach to the Technical Assessment. What did you check first, any assumptions, and what resources were used?" |
| 10–40 min | Deep dives (3 × ~10 min each) |
| 40–45 min | Wrap-up & candidate questions |

**After the interview:** ~15 minutes to enter feedback into iCIMS

**Questions covered:**
- C2C: Q1 CI/CD Pipeline
- Worktent: Q5 Unauthorized message when using Copilot in VS Code
- GHES: Q7 Sudden Disk Growth – First Diagnostic Steps

### Scoring

There are 12 points possible for each of 3 questions (4 criteria × 1–3 points each).

**Criteria:**
- Understanding & Reasoning
- Diagnostic Methodology & Next Steps
- Safety & Risk Awareness
- Communication & Customer Skills

**Score Bands:**
- 31-36 (84-100%) = Exceptional → Hire
- 25-30 (69-82%) = Meets Expectations → Hire
- 0-24 (<67%) = Does Not Meet Expectations → Do Not Hire

For full probing questions and good answer examples, see the [Technical Debrief Run Sheet](technical-debrief/runsheet.md).
