# Enterprise Support Manager Interview Process

## Interview Sequence
- [Cover letter and resume review](#cover-letter-and-resume-review)
- [First interview](#first-interview)
- [Second interview](#second-interview)
- [Final round interview](#final-round-interview)

## Cover Letter and Resume Review
Candidates should supply a cover letter in addition to a resume. Ideally, the cover letter will offer enough information to make a decision to move forward to the first interview. If the cover letter gives insufficient information to make a decision, or no cover letter is supplied, the candidate should not be moved forward. The primary reasoning here is that a candidate should be able to communicate clearly in writing, and resumes tend to introduce bias in many areas. Where the candidate went to school and where they've worked, for example, introduce bias without helping make useful decisions.

### Reviewing a cover letter
When reading a cover letter, these are the primary things to look for:
- Clarity in written communication
- Experience managing people
- Passion for managing support professionals
- Focus on customer service
- Ability to work cross functionally with various teams


If the candidate seems simply excited about working at GitHub, and does not specifically talk about support, customer service, and people management at all, they should be rejected.

## First Interview
The first conversation with a candidate should cover some basic areas regarding managing a support team, customer service, and escalation management.  The first interview should be completed by the hiring manager.

### Time
45 minutes

### Prep
The interviewer should prepare by
- Reviewing the candidate's cover letter and resume
- Reviewing the interview questions

### Interview introduction
Begin the interview by introducing yourself and your role at the company. Talk briefly about the purpose of the Enterprise Support team and the Enterprise Support Manager role. Points to highlight:
- The Enterprise Support team is part of the larger Support organization, focused on GitHub Enterprise customers.
- The Enterprise Support team provides support for the products available to our Enterprise customers. 
- The manager role will be building this team (with the help of the other Support managers and the Director of Enterprise Support).
- The manager will guide and facilitate the work of the team, which requires personnel management and customer service skills.

This should take no more than three minutes or so, but it helps give some context for the candidate.

### Interview questions and expected answers
- What motivates you to work with customers?
  - __Good__
    - Likes working directly with customers.
    - Actively listen and ask questions to help resolve an issue.
    - A friendly voice/face to a customer while solving problems.
  - __Bad__
    - Prefers to work alone or interact with few people
    - Is unable to listen and be empathetic
    - Inability to understanding how an issue can impact the customer's business

- What motivates you to manage a support team?
  - __Good__
    - Helping others succeed.
    - Coaching and mentoring.
    - Understanding customer needs and requirements for service.
  - __Bad__
    - I have the best ideas and know how things should be done.
    - It's the only next step in the career.

- What are some of the key performance indicators for managing a support team?
  - __Good__
    - Call hold times, # of incoming calls, call abandonment rate.
    - Initial time to respond.
    - Time to resolution, first call resolution.
    - Customer surveys.
    - Incoming tickets, tickets closed, trending backlog.
  - __Bad__
    - Having no idea what types of metrics are reviewed for phone and ticket support.
    - Seeing no value in customer feedback.
    - Can't speak to quality of service.
    - No understanding of ticket quality reviews.

- What is your view on the positives/negatives of working on and managing remote teams?
  - __Good__
    - Remote teams are just as effective as local teams.
    - Communication tools are vital to the success of remote teams.
    - Managers can really help remote people feel more connected and included.
    - Can explain how remote teams have different factors than local teams that
      affect effectiveness. For example, serial tasks might sometimes take
      longer (due to the turnaround time for feedback), but remote teams can
      offset that by working on many parallel tasks.
    - Can articulate strategies for ameliorating challenges due to different
      factors that affect remote teams. For example, remote teams located in the
      same timezone might have different challenges than those in a wide range
      of timezones.
  - __Bad__
    - Doesn't believe remote teams can be effective.
    - Remote people need more baby-sitting.

- What is your approach to hiring and on-boarding new engineers?
  - __Good__
    - Support specialists need to be part of the interview process to assess technical
      excellence.
    - On-boarding needs to be well-planned in order to be successful and here
      are some examples...
  - __Bad__
    - I usually just hire people I've worked with in the past.
    - I go with the "throw them into the deep end" approach to on-boarding.

- What do you do differently for junior level support engineers?
  - __Good__
    - I make sure they are on teams with senior engineers who are committed to
      mentoring.
    - I identify projects that allow them grow and level up.
    - I prepare the team for their addition so it isn't disruptive.
  - __Bad__
    - I avoid hiring them.
    - I make sure they "stay out of the way".
    - I make jokes about how Millenials are ruining everything just as my
      forebears did about my generation.

- In this role, you'll be hiring support engineers into the company. What steps can you
  take to create a diverse and inclusive team?
  - __Good__
    - Concrete examples of work they've done in the past.
    - Examples of work they've seen be successful elsewhere.
    - Thoughtful discussion of the current issues of diversity and inclusion in
      the tech community.
  - __Bad__
    - They haven't thought much about this until now.
    - They don't think diversity/inclusion is a problem.
    - They don't think tech companies should worry about diversity/inclusion.

## Second Interview
This interview digs deeper into technical taste and managerial judgement. The second interview should be done by a Support Manager. If we are introducing a new manager specific to location, APAC, US, EU, the manager who will be working within the same timezone should be part of the second interview. 

- What steps would you take to manage a customer escalation?
  - __Good__
    - Understand the customer business requirements and assign the appropriate priorty/support personnel to the ticket.
    - Regular follow-up with the customer and support person working on the issue.
    - Bring in higher level of management, engineering, etc. depending on the severity of the issue.
  - __Bad__
    - Avoid contacting the customer on plan to assist with escalation.
    - Deny the customer escalation.
    - Just let the level phone personnel handle difficult customer situations.

- Provide details about support tiers/levels or types of support offerings?
  - __Good__
    - Give examples of support tiers/levels and functions.
    - Examples of managing support through tickets vs. phone vs. dedicated types of services.
    - Additional information on creative ways to support customers, i.e. through chat, window shares, etc.
  - __Bad__
    - Unable to describe tiers/levels, support offerings, or processes to support managing a support business.
    - Unable to walk through an example of a support process flow.

- What skills are important in a customer support position?
  - __Good__
    - Patience, attentiveness, positive language.
    - Clear written and oral communication.
    - Knowledge of the product.
    - Ability to manage time.
    - Goal orientation.
    - Willingness to learn.
  - __Bad__
    - Poor customer skills.
    - Inability to ask questions and gather data.
    - Unempathetic to customer concerns.

- What is the ideal amount of troubleshooting and analysis you would be doing as a manager on support tickets? 80/20? 50/50?
  - __Good__
    - Less than 50%.
    - Understands that the manager's role is to help the support team do their best work, not to do the work for them.
    - Is willing to get "get one's hands dirty" when necessary, but prefers to coach rather than substitute.
  - __Bad__
    - Wants to spend a lot of time working tickets, rather than helping others succeed at it.
    - Doesn't want to spend any time looking at tickets at all, sees it as a waste of time.

* How do you add value to your teams?
  * **Good**
    * Touches upon some of the following:
      * Build trust
      * Foster highly functional teams/teamwork
      * Support career development
      * Make sure people are challenged and rewarded
      * Hire top people
      * Prevent silos and isolation
      * Removing obstacles for my team
      * Communicate and align team to company vision/goals
  * **Great**
    * Hits on many if not most of the of the topics in the Good section.
  * **Bad**
    * Scope of value add seems small and not aligned to company.
    * Correcting engineers' mistakes by rewriting ticket entries.
    * Something that smells like micro-managing.
    * Telling people what to work on.

* Describe your style of people management.
  * **Good**
    * A clear answer that communicates well the candidate's style and includes
      specifics on many if not most of the following:
         * Empathy
         * Clear communication
         * Fosters personal growth
         * Helps with coordination
         * Fosters autonomy where needed
         * Provides coaching where needed
         * Challenges people
         * Involves people in decision making
         * Creates culture of accountability
         * Delegates
  * **Bad**
    * Does not have a style they can articulate coherently.
    * Creates the impression Dilbert's boss might be based on the candidate.
    * Tells people what to do/micro-manages.
    * Drives people too hard.
    * Never admits mistakes.
    * Creates culture of blame.
    * Creates culture of competition. For example, pits employees in caged death
      matches or other zero sum games that quelch cooperation.
    * Fails to delegate.
    * Describes a vague and/or cliche-ed style that includes things like:
      "shoot from the hip", "take it one game at a time", "managing is like the
      wind, you only feel it when it is moving".

* What don't you like about managing?
  * **Good**
    * Describes specific past challenges faced.
    * Discusses how they mitigate aspects they don't like.
  * **Bad**
    * Terse "I like everything about it" answer with no elaboration.
    * Doing 1-1s and/or performance reviews.
    * Mentoring/coaching people.
    * People in general.

* Tell me about the most diverse team you've worked on. How did you create effective working relationships with your team members and what challenges did you encounter in doing so?**
  * **Awesome** (i.e. Lever 4 score)
    * Bring in a personal example (work, class, volunteer, community).
    * Clear example, noting comfort describing the team's demographics. Clear articulation about the challenges and benefits of this team.
  * **Great** (i.e. Lever 3 score)
    * Talking on a theoretical level (read a book, movie, class, etc.).
    * Passionate, genuine, curious.
  * **OK** (i.e. Lever 2 score)
    * Come across as less genuine or enthusiastic about the topic.
    * Demonstrated desire to be part of a diverse team, regardless of past experience.
  * **Not So Great** (i.e. Lever 1 score)
    * “It isn’t a concern of mine.”
    * Lack of interest/dismissive about the topic.
    * No interest, resistance to the question, negative expression about how it would be to work on a diverse team.

## Final Round Interview

The interviewers will cover these topics:
- [Team dynamics](#team-dynamics)
- [People management](#people-management)
- [Communication](#communication)
- [Working with distributed teams](#working-with-distributed-teams)
- [Diversity and inclusiveness](#diversity-and-inclusiveness)

### Team Dynamics
* How do you keep a team focused on evolving company goals?
  * **Good**
    * I use my 1-1s to make sure people understand the goals and the reasoning
      behind them.
    * I make sure their feedback is heard and that they have input on the
    strategy.
    * I make sure to communicate short term goals and how they relate to longer
      term goals.
    * I follow up and show how completed work related to past goals to encourage
      a feeling of accomplishment before moving onto the next big task.
  * **Great**
    * Apply pressure upwards to communicate a clear vision and purpose. Then
      communicate that downward and help connect current tasks/goals to the
      company's goals.
    * Communicate how the work individuals on the team are doing further the
      team's goals and as such, further company's goals.
    * Communicate a sense of purpose to the work we do that's meaningful to
      members of the team.  
  * **Bad**
    * I tell them not to think about it, just do what you are told.
    * I clue them into my personal master plan to overthrow the current regime
      ala House of Cards.

* How do you get commitment from a team?
  * **Good**
    * Make sure it is clear and documented how our projects may help achieve
      the company goals.
    * Ensure each member of the team understands individual responsibilities
      and are in the right roles to accomplish our goals.
    * Build trust with the team and foster trust with the team and other groups
      in the company.
  * **Great**
    * Empowerment and Ownership: Once goals and roles/responsibilities are set,
      I give the team ownership in how they accomplish our objectives and hold
      them accountable to those expectations.
    * Make sure each member of the team feels supported in accomplishing their
      expectations.
    * Consistently provide recognition when they accomplish their commitments.
  * **Bad**
    * Tell them they might be let go if they don't "play ball".
    * Rely on them to just sort it all out and get annoyed when they need my
      help.
    * I purchase commitment rings and ask everyone to wear them in a commitment
      ceremony.

* How do you encourage teamwork over individual interests?
  * **Good**
    * Encourage working together (such as pairing) to make sure multiple people
      are on-board with the approach being taken.
    * Communicate approaches in a team forum that allows others to contribute
      feedback.
    * Prevent silos by making sure team members are collaborating on and handing off tickets.
    * Encourage team members to reach out to other teams for feedback and assistance on a technical review.
  * **Great**
    * Foster trust by being trustworthy.
    * Really listen to team ideas and try to hold back on injecting own ideas
      into the conversation initially. Feel free to ask clarifying questions
      and introduce ideas if the team doesn't consider them on their own.
    * Foster trust amongst team members. Provides specifics on how to do that.
    * Facilitate conflict management among team members. Focus on having them
      work through it, with manager guidance.
  * **Bad**
    * Something that smells like micro-managing.

* How do you resolve conflict within the team?
  * **Good**
    * Recognize it early and don't let it fester and grow.
    * Mediate discussions between team members to make sure they are healthy,
      productive, and focused.
    * Focus on helping team members feel heard by the other side first, before
      jumping into problem solving.
    * Encourage team members to problem solve.
    * Lean on senior engineers for adding context and input.
  * **Great**
    * Attempt to separate intent from impact and understand each side's position
      thoroughly.
    * Recognizes that humans are emotional beings and emotions have to be dealt
      with, not swept under the rug.
    * Understands people never change their minds without first feeling like
      they've been heard.
    * Understands not everyone will always agree with an outcome, but as long
      as they feel the process has been fair and they've been heard, that's ok.
  * **Bad**
    * Just wait it out. Ignore it. It'll be fiiiiiine.
    * Jump in and take a side too early.
    * Tries to focus on just the facts and get the emotions out of the way.
* Give examples where you’ve had to make a difficult decision and been the arbiter between two team members

### People Management
* How do you motivate high performers? How would you make high performers feel
  motivated and appreciated?
  * **Good**
    * Increased leadership role.
    * Provide autonomy and accountability.
    * More challenging projects and ambitious goals.
    * Help them level up in new areas.
    * Opportunities to mentor people.
    * Make sure they are paid well and have great equity.
    * Make sure they are taking enough time off to recharge.
    * Give praise and recognition of the impact they have on the organization. Show them they are valued.
    * Provide opportunties for people to see the results of their work such as
      being in contact with the beneficiaries.
  * **Bad**
    * Telling them they aren't that great so they work harder.
    * I've never worked with high performers before, everyone is terrible.

* How do you deal with low performers?
  * **Good**
    * Try to find the root cause of why they aren't productive.
    * Make sure they have a clear understanding of what is expected of them and
      in what specific ways they are not meeting expectations.
    * Get them additional mentoring or resources that will help them level up.
  * **Bad**
    * Fire them because they aren't worth the time.
    * Try to pawn them off onto a different team.

* How do you approach 1:1s?
  * **Good**
    * Make sure they are on a regular cadence at least every 2 weeks.
    * Inform them of forthcoming changes that may impact them.
    * Have an agenda, check in on career growth goals, identify roadblocks
      and inter-personal issues.
  * **Better**
    * Use them to build trust with individuals as individuals.
    * Have them weekly.
  * **Bad**
    * I detest them.
    * You only need them once a month.
    * We just talk about whatever.
    * I ask them to talk about what they've worked on in the last week.

* Give an example where you’ve helped someone achieve their next level of career growth

* How do you consider the situation where you have an excellent engineer who
  frequently garners complaints from his/her team members regarding their being
  difficult to work with?
  * **Good**
    * Be sensitive and empathetic to both those giving complaints and the
      subject of the complaints.
    * Try to find the root cause of the complaints. Are they legitimate? Is bias
      involved.
    * Attempt to mediate a candid discussion with those expressing complaints
      and the subject. Focus on problem solving, not blame.
  * **Bad**
    * Assumes that the subject of the complaints must be difficult to work with.
    * Ignores the problem. Hopes it goes away.

### Communication
* How do you communicate generally, how do you run the team communication wise?
  * **Good**
    * Make sure all voices are heard.
    * Be asynchronous whenever possible.
    * Always be accessible.
  * **Bad**
    * I only email.
    * Chat rooms scare me.
    * Always be synchronous.
    * Just the facts, no feelings please.

* How do you expect those on your team to communicate with you and how do you
  communicate those expectations?
  * **Good**
    * In the way they want, I'm flexible.
    * I make sure we discuss it in our initial 1-1 to set expectations.
  * **Bad**
    * My way or the highway.

### Working with Distributed Teams
* What’s your experience with distributed teams?
  * **Good**
    * Prior experience managing distributed or co-located teams.
  * **Bad**
    * "Isn't a fan" of distributed teams.

* How many timezones have you dealt with at once?
  * **Good**
    * More than one.
    * Mentions it has its benefits such as on call rotations.
    * Provides examples of how they "made it work".
    * Is able to articlate the "follow the sun" approach to managing work across timezones.
  * **Bad**
    * Not excited to deal with timezones.
    * Mentions wanting to hiring only people in their own timezone.

* Have you done 1:1s over the phone or video chat in the past? How has that worked for you?
  * **Good**
    * Has done them in the past or is willing to.
    * They get the job done just as well as in-person if you do them right.
    * Provides interesting examples of techniques related to this.
  * **Bad**
    * Anything that sounds like they use them as a status update as opposed to
      building trust and provide a forum for employee to raise concerns.
    * Prefers walking over to someone's desk and tapping them on the shoulder.

### Diversity and Inclusiveness
* What kind of involvement have you had with Community Engagement or Corporate Social Responsibility efforts around philanthropy or volunteerism?**
    * **Awesome** (i.e. Lever 4 score)
      * Significant involvement, offering specific examples.
      * Has demonstrated action, lead efforts, significant amount of experience participating. Actively informed on issues in the community.
      * Ex: Passionate about change within the company. Head of LGBT for global efforts for Intuit.
    * **Great** (i.e. Lever 3 score)
      * Participated and provides examples but isn’t necessarily spearheading efforts.
      * Demonstrated interest and ideas, irrespective of experience.
    * **OK** (i.e. Lever 2 score)
      * Hasn’t done anything specific but may have interest.
      * Example: Talked about the program at their company. But didn’t talk about specific involvement.
    * **Not So Great** (i.e. Lever 1 score)
      * No awareness. Or actively thinks it’s a waste of time.
      * “Doesn’t see how that pertains to their job".
      * No interest or a clear resistance to these initiatives, regardless of position for which the candidate is interviewing.

* How do you ensure diversity in a quickly growing team?
  * **Awesome** (i.e. Lever 4 score)
    * Make it a priority.
    * Opportunity should be made available to people of any race or gender. It shows there's a career path for marginalized people and nothing is more powerful than seeing people who look like themselves in positions of power.
    * Provide diversity and unconscious bias training for employees and managers.
    * Implement systems of recruiting and hiring that remove bias from the process (similar to blind auditions)
  * **Great**
    * Proactively source diverse candidates
    * Get involved and support professional groups that focus on creating opportunities for all races and gender.
  * **OK** (i.e. Lever 2 score)
    * I haven't thought about it, but I'd like to learn and be a part of the solution.
  * **Not So Great** (i.e. Lever 1 Score)
    * It's not an issue.
    * It's not important.
    * I haven't thought about it.
